## I. The Polish Situation, 1815–1830

After the fall of Napoleon, the Congress of Vienna (1815) redrew the map of Europe. A small Polish state was carved out of the former Duchy of Warsaw and given the name **Kingdom of Poland**, also known as the Congress Kingdom. It existed in personal union with the Russian Empire: the Tsar of Russia was simultaneously the King of Poland.

On paper, the Kingdom enjoyed an extraordinary degree of autonomy. The Constitution of 1815, granted by Tsar Alexander I, preserved a Polish parliament (the Sejm), a Polish army, Polish-language education, the Polish złoty, and a separate civil service. The Catholic Church retained its status. Warsaw, the capital, retained its university (founded 1816), its theatres, its courts.

In practice, this autonomy was eroded steadily across fifteen years. Censorship tightened. The Sejm's powers were curtailed. A Russian-run secret police network, headed by senator Nikolai Novosiltsev, monitored Polish citizens — particularly students, officers, and clergy. By 1825, when Tsar Alexander died and his brother Nicholas I came to the throne, the gap between constitutional theory and lived reality had become impossible to ignore.

The Tsar's brother, **Grand Duke Constantine Pavlovich**, served as commander-in-chief of the Polish army and the de facto strongman of the Kingdom. Constantine was a complicated figure. He had renounced his claim to the Russian throne in 1822 to marry a Polish noblewoman, **Joanna Grudzińska** (later Princess of Łowicz), and he developed a genuine attachment to Warsaw. But his rule was capricious and harsh. He humiliated Polish officers in public. He punished real and imagined infractions with brutal parade-ground discipline. Several officers committed suicide rather than endure his treatment. Yet he could also be charming, generous, and surprisingly loyal to those he favored.

**Game implication:** Constantine is a real character the player will encounter. He should never be simplified into a villain. He is a man with affections, contradictions, and bad temper, who happens to be the instrument of an occupying power.

## II. Society

### The Szlachta (Nobility)

The Polish nobility was unlike that of other European countries. At its height in the 18th century it comprised perhaps 8–10% of the population — far higher than in France or England. By 1830, hereditary nobility still defined the political nation, even as economic reality eroded the lower ranks.

Three rough strata exist for our purposes:

- **Magnates** — extremely wealthy aristocratic families with vast estates, palaces in Warsaw, and political networks that often extended into both the Russian court and Western Europe. The Czartoryski, Radziwiłł, Potocki, Zamoyski, Sapieha families are real examples. Antoni Sobański (player character) belongs in this stratum, though as a younger son of a junior branch.
- **Middle nobility (szlachta średnia)** — owners of one or several villages, with the legal privileges of a noble but more modest means. They lived on their estates, came to Warsaw for the Sejm season or for daughters' debuts, and supplied much of the officer corps and civil service.
- **Impoverished nobility (szlachta zaściankowa, zubożała szlachta)** — families who retained their noble title but had lost most or all of their land, sometimes living little better than peasants. Marianne's family belongs at the upper edge of this stratum: they still own their manor and a handful of villages, but they have mortgaged repeatedly, lost land in the Napoleonic upheavals, and cannot match the dowries their daughters' suitors expect.

A crucial fact: nobility was hereditary and could not be legally lost (except by formal sentence). A poor szlachcic was still a szlachcic, with the right to vote in regional assemblies, the right to a coat of arms, and the social entitlement to use the title *Pan*.

### The Mieszczaństwo (Townsmen)

The townsman class was rising. Warsaw in 1830 had perhaps 140,000 inhabitants and growing. The professional bourgeoisie — doctors, lawyers, apothecaries, engineers, merchants — was acquiring economic power without the legal privileges of the szlachta. Some converted their wealth into noble status by purchasing land and lobbying for ennoblement. Others built influence within their own world: the medical academy, the bar, the merchant guilds.

Tadeusz Lipiński (player character, the doctor) is a representative of this class — son of an apothecary, educated in medicine at the University of Warsaw and Berlin, ambitious to be received in noble salons. The tension between his birth and his aspirations is one of the core dramatic engines of his storyline.

### The Chłopi (Peasants)

The peasantry — overwhelmingly the largest class — were mostly serfs on private estates, with limited rights of movement and labor obligations to the landlord. On royal and ecclesiastical land their position was somewhat better. The Polish Sejm had not abolished serfdom (the Napoleonic Code of the Duchy of Warsaw had nominally freed peasants but left them landless). Reform was a recurring topic of political debate but politically dangerous.

Peasants are largely background figures in the game's main story but feature in the rural locations and in the Doctor's questline (cholera fears, traditional vs. academic medicine).

### The Jewish Community

Warsaw's Jewish community numbered roughly 30,000 in 1830 and was concentrated in the northern districts (today Muranów). They were excluded from many trades and from owning land within city walls but were active in commerce, finance, and crafts. Jewish characters can appear as merchants, money-lenders, physicians, or in the medical setting (Tadeusz had Jewish classmates at university). The game treats them with period-accurate complexity — neither caricature nor anachronistic uplift.

### The Catholic Church

Poland was overwhelmingly Catholic. Parish priests were a stabilizing force in rural life. The Church was officially loyal to the Tsar but contained currents of Polish patriotism, particularly in the lower clergy. Bishops were political figures, often torn between Rome, the Polish nation, and the imperial administration.

## III. Patriotic Currents

The memory of partition was alive. Most adults in 1830 remembered the Duchy of Warsaw, the Polish Legions in Napoleon's army, the brief flicker of independence. Older men remembered the Kościuszko Uprising of 1794. The hope that Poland might be restored — by reform, by uprising, or by foreign war — never died.

Several currents fed this hope:

**Romanticism.** Adam Mickiewicz published *Konrad Wallenrod* in 1828 — ostensibly about a medieval Lithuanian hero, in reality an allegory of resistance through dissimulation. It was widely read in salons and student circles. The book is referenced in the game as a coded text shared between conspirators.

**Secret societies.** The Patriotic Society had been broken up in the early 1820s after the so-called Trial of the Patriots, but informal networks persisted. Free Mason lodges — though officially dissolved in 1821 — had created cross-class friendships that were politically useful.

**The Cadet School conspiracy.** The most consequential conspiracy of 1830 was organized in the Officer Cadet Infantry School (Szkoła Podchorążych Piechoty), led by lieutenant **Piotr Wysocki**. The conspirators were young officers and cadets, perhaps two hundred at the inner circle, with sympathizers across the army and civilian intelligentsia. Their plan: a coordinated attack on Belweder Palace (Constantine's residence), the seizure of the Saxon Square arsenal, the killing or capture of unpopular Russian-aligned generals, and a general uprising to follow.

The conspiracy was not a popular movement. The conspirators distrusted the older political class and feared betrayal. Their plan was triggered earlier than intended, on **the night of November 29, 1830**, after rumors that arrests were imminent.

**Game implication:** Wysocki and several of his real lieutenants appear in the game. Players may interact with the conspiracy from inside (as Tadeusz, drawn in by his mentor) or from outside (as Marianne, learning of it through her brother) or hostile to it (as Sobański, attempting to manage or suppress it).

## IV. Autumn 1830

The summer of 1830 brought political shocks from the West. In late July, **revolution broke out in Paris** and the Bourbon king Charles X was overthrown. In August, **revolution broke out in Brussels** against Dutch rule. Tsar Nicholas viewed both events with horror. He convened his ministers, ordered the Russian army onto a war footing, and announced his intention to use the Polish army (along with Russian troops) to march westward and crush the Belgian rebels.

For Polish officers, the order was unbearable. The Polish Constitution forbade the army's use outside the Kingdom without Sejm consent. The cause — crushing fellow revolutionaries fighting for national independence, with whom Poles felt deep sympathy — was repugnant. The Cadet conspirators concluded that they had to act before mobilization began.

Through October and November, tension accumulated. Constantine sensed something but dismissed reports. The Russian general Włodzimierz Rożniecki (head of the gendarmerie, hated by Polish officers) was on the conspirators' kill list. Foreign news traveled slowly but constantly. Salons whispered. Students gathered. The harvest came in. The first cold wind blew across the Vistula.

The game's "present" begins in **mid-September 1830** and runs through **early December**. The player's choices accumulate against this rising tide.

## V. The Night of November 29

(*A summary, for narrative reference. The specific in-game depiction is in the quest design document QD-12.*)

In the early evening of November 29, a small group of cadets set fire to a brewery on Solec — the agreed signal. A larger group attacked Belweder Palace from the gardens. Constantine, warned just in time, escaped through a side door in his dressing gown; one of his adjutants was killed in the scuffle. Other groups attacked the Russian cavalry barracks and the Saxon Square arsenal. By midnight, much of central Warsaw was in conspirator hands. The civilian population, surprised, was variously hostile, supportive, or simply terrified. Several Russian-aligned Polish generals — Stanisław Trębicki, Maurycy Hauke, Józef Nowicki — were killed in the streets, some by mistake.

The Uprising had begun, but its leadership had not yet assembled. The conspirators turned to senior figures — General Józef Chłopicki, the Patriotic Council, eventually Adam Czartoryski — who had not been part of the conspiracy and were ambivalent about it. The next weeks were as politically chaotic as they were militarily decisive.

The game's main story ends the morning of November 30. Epilogue scenes depict each character's fate in the weeks and years following. (See the epilogue branching document.)

## VI. World Customs and Daily Life

### Calendar

The Kingdom uses the Gregorian calendar; Russia uses the Julian. Russian official documents in Warsaw are double-dated. In game text, all dates are Gregorian unless explicitly marked.

### Money

The Polish złoty (zł) circulates alongside the Russian ruble. One ruble equals roughly 6.6 złoty. A skilled craftsman might earn 4–6 złoty a day; a bottle of decent wine costs around 3 złoty; a noble's annual income (middle szlachta) might be 5,000–15,000 złoty.

### Travel

Within Warsaw: foot, hired hackney carriage (dorożka), private carriage. Between cities: post-stage coaches on the main highways (the road to Berlin via Poznań is the main westward route). The Vistula remains an important route for goods. A journey from Warsaw to Cracow takes roughly four days by coach.

### Forms of address

- **Pan / Pani** — the ordinary respectful address for adult men/women, used between strangers and across ranks. Default for any noble or burgher in dialogue.
- **Jaśnie Wielmożny / Jaśnie Pan** — formal address for high nobility, used by inferiors.
- **Wasza Wysokość** — Your Highness, for Constantine.
- **Wasza Cesarska Mość** — Your Imperial Majesty, for the Tsar (used only in correspondence).
- **Mości Panie / Mościa Pani** — old-fashioned, slightly archaizing, used by the older generation.

### Common period vocabulary the player will hear

*Kontusz* (traditional Polish noble overcoat — older men still wear it for formal occasions); *pańszczyzna* (serf labor); *karbowy* (estate overseer); *austeria* (inn); *gazeta* (newspaper); *szlachcic / szlachcianka* (nobleman / noblewoman); *Moskal* (a derogatory term for Russians, common in patriotic speech); *germanizator* (a derogatory term for those favoring Germanic culture).

### Dueling and the code

Dueling between gentlemen is illegal but tolerated, particularly in the army. The code is unwritten but understood: an offense to honor is answered with a challenge through seconds; the choice of weapon falls to the offended party; sabre is the standard weapon among Polish officers, pistol among civilians and the more cosmopolitan set. First blood usually closes a sabre duel. Pistols at twenty paces, one shot each, is the common pistol form. Antoni has fought twice (one of them notorious); Stefan has not but is widely expected to.

### Personal weapons in daily life

Polish noblemen routinely wear a sabre at their side at any formal occasion and on the road. A sword cane is increasingly fashionable for city wear among younger men who find an open sabre old-fashioned. Pistols are kept in the home for defense and travel — generally a single flintlock or, among the wealthier, a percussion-cap pistol of recent manufacture. (The percussion cap, introduced in the 1820s, is the new technology of the period; the older flintlock is more reliable in the rain.) Townsmen carry knives openly, gentlemen do not. Officers may carry military-issue pistols off duty.

### Hunting

The autumn hunt is a fixture of noble social life. Marianne attends the Wilanów hunt in Act II as part of the Sobański courtship. Hunting weapons are smoothbore fowling pieces (for birds) and rifled hunting guns (for boar and deer). Hunting is a context where social hierarchies relax slightly — a junior officer may ride next to a magnate — and where private conversations become possible.

### A note on medicine

Polish medicine in 1830 stands between two worlds. The University of Warsaw teaches modern clinical observation, percussion of the chest, the Hippocratic tradition refined through Hufeland and the German universities. Old practice — bleeding, purging, the four humours — is still routine in rural areas and among older physicians. Apothecaries compound their own remedies; laudanum, mercury preparations, and various herbal tinctures are routinely dispensed. Surgery is brutal and pre-anaesthetic; ether will arrive in 1846. The 1831 cholera epidemic, advancing west from the Russian Empire through 1830, is on every educated physician's mind. Tadeusz's storyline depends on this background being legible.

## VII. Legends and Stories Referenced In-Game

### The Black Madonna of Częstochowa

The icon at the Jasna Góra monastery in Częstochowa is the most revered religious image in Poland. The legend of its defense against Swedish forces in 1655 is foundational to the Polish Catholic-patriotic identity. A character may swear "by the Black Madonna" or vow a pilgrimage in moments of crisis.

### The Legend of Wars and Sawa

The mythological founding of Warsaw: a fisherman named Wars and a mermaid named Sawa. The mermaid (*syrena*) is the city's symbol and appears on the city arms. NPCs in dockside areas may reference her.

### The Twardowski legend

Pan Twardowski, a 16th-century Faust figure who tricked the devil. A folk tale referenced in popular speech.

### The legend of Princess Wanda

A Cracovian princess who drowned herself in the Vistula rather than marry a German prince. A patriotic-feminine archetype, sometimes invoked by or about Marianne.
